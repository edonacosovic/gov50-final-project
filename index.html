Introduction The central question I would like to investigate is what kind of effect do asylum seekers have with arrests in 2023? In this study, I take two data sets that have data within the time frame January 2023 to June 2023. The first data set explores the number of arrests carried out by the NYPD. The second data set explores the number of asylum reports initiated by the United States Department of Homeland Security. With this data, I explore the relationship between Arrests and Asylum Reports. I hypothesized that there is going to be an increased number of arrests made in conjunction with asylum reports. This is because I expect that in big cities like NYC that bear the brunt of asylum seekers, there could be a shift in public sentiment about the local government’s policy and thus more people committing crimes. With this, it would be interesing to see the crime fluctuations as a result of NYC’s constant commitment to immigrants (via sanctuary city status).

Data I have two data sets received from two different sources. The first is data I retrieved from the Department of Homeland Security and Department of Justice in regards to Asylum Processing Rule Cohort Reports from January to June of 2023. The second data I retrieved from the New York Police Department regarding the arrests in the same period of time.

The Asylum Processing Rule Cohort Reports data that I used combined all the “Credible Fear Claim Outcomes” which essentially means people that stated that they were claiming asylum as a result of credible fears. Thus, you will see that the both positive and negative fear claims were recorded. The data was recorded from the information they gathered from judges and courts in order to probably gain insights on the outcomes of these credible fear claims, which is also likely designed to discourage fraudulent asylum claims. However, this information can importantly be used to determine the general trend of asylum seekers/credible immigration seekers into the US.

Conclusion In conclusion, my data might imply some negative correlation between the number of arrests and number of asylum reports– while arrests increase, the number of processed asylum reports decrease – but the results are statistically insignificant. Thus, we cannot assume that there is any causal relationship and be wary to take this correlation as true. Instead, we should opt for some more data to test the statistical significance of that information. This information does not support my hypothesis, but it doesn’t mean that it is necessarily incorrect – there’s just not information to make a solid conclusion on it right now.

However, there are some significant limitations in my data regarding the time frame. Due to the datasets being limited in terms of scope over time as well as not both being in NYC, this case-specific negative correlation cannot simply imply causation. This negative slope is merely suggesting that there is a case-specific statistical tendence for the two variables to move in opposite directions. Other reasons that could be skewing this is attempts to reduce crime have been heightened in NYC while asylum cases remain relatively unaffected – skewing the results. To fix this, I could try to have more data points by using a wider time range and maybe try to find more numbers regarding immigration trends in NYC specifically rather than asylum cases processed federally. Thus, there could be complex other factors impacting the data. Due to the fact that it only includes data from the last 6 months, we can see that the data is pretty limited and current events on crime deterrence efforts can be skewing the data. By finding more data points, we can see if this relationship is moreso geared towards randomness or underlying relationship. Additionally, if I found asylum cases strictly done in the New York City region, that would increase the accuracy of the results even more.
